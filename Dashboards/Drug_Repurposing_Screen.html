Drug Repurposing Screen for Diabetes

Pressure-tested screen of 34 generic drugs with diabetes-relevant mechanisms for equity interventions in low-resource settings

What This Dashboard Answers

Most diabetes drug development targets novel molecules with decade-long timelines and high-income-country pricing. Meanwhile, dozens of cheap, globally available generic drugs have mechanisms relevant to diabetes pathophysiology — anti-inflammatory, beta cell protective, immunomodulatory — that have never been systematically evaluated for repurposing. This screen asks: which existing generics have the strongest mechanistic, safety, and equity case for diabetes intervention, and which could be deployed in low-resource settings where novel therapies will not arrive for years?

How to Use This

For researchers: the ranked table identifies which drugs warrant pilot studies in specific diabetes contexts (LADA, islet transplant, T2D inflammation). For funders: the equity score highlights drugs with the best cost-to-impact ratio in LMICs. For policymakers: the WHO Essential Medicines flags show which drugs are already in global supply chains and could be repurposed without new procurement infrastructure. This is a prioritization tool for deciding where to invest limited research dollars, not a clinical recommendation.

Validation Standards

Every drug in this screen has been pressure-tested. WHO Essential Medicines flags are verified against the 2023 WHO Model List. Mechanism claims are checked against cited PMIDs — negative trials (e.g., ENDIT for nicotinamide) are explicitly labeled as negative. Preclinical-only evidence (e.g., lithium, disulfiram) is clearly distinguished from human RCT data. Partially validated mechanisms (e.g., verapamil TXNIP in rodents only) carry qualifying language. Scores reflect verified evidence, not promotional claims.

What This Cannot Tell You

Composite scores are weighted estimates, not clinical endpoints. A high-scoring drug may still fail in a specific population, disease stage, or healthcare context. Drug-drug interactions, local regulatory approval, and clinical infrastructure are not modeled. The equity score assumes that cost and global availability are the primary barriers — this may understate cultural, regulatory, or logistical barriers in specific countries.

Executive Summary

This computational screen evaluates 34 generic drugs across five dimensions (mechanism relevance 25%, safety profile 20%, generic availability 20%, evidence strength 20%, equity score 15%) to identify existing agents with mechanisms pertinent to diabetes pathophysiology that could serve as low-cost interventions in resource-limited settings.

Key findings: (1) 27 candidates cost under $1/month with relevant mechanisms for both T2D and autoimmune diabetes (Hernandez 2018, PMID:29710129), (2) NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitors emerge as leading candidates across 4 drugs including glyburide (pennies/month, WHO Essential), (3) beta cell preservation is achievable through verapamil ($0.15/month, RCT-proven in T1D; Hernandez 2018, PMID:29710129) and cyclosporine (RCT-proven but toxicity-limited), and (4) 14 drugs are already WHO Essential Medicines with existing global supply chains.

Important caveats: nicotinamide scored high on mechanism and cost but the landmark ENDIT trial was negative for T1D prevention. Hydroxychloroquine insulin sensitivity claims lack RCT support. Lithium beta cell proliferation is preclinical only. All evidence qualifiers are noted in individual drug entries below.

Methodology

Scoring Framework

Each drug is scored across five dimensions, with a composite score calculated as a weighted average:

Mechanism Relevance (25%)
How comprehensively does this drug's mechanism address known diabetes pathophysiology? Diabetes involves multiple overlapping pathways: glycemic control (glucose metabolism), insulin secretion (beta cell function), insulin sensitivity (GLUT4 translocation, AKT signaling), inflammation (NLRP3 inflammasome, TNF-alpha, IL-6), autoimmunity (in T1D/LADA), lipotoxicity, and endothelial dysfunction. Agents hitting 3+ pathways score 9-10; agents with focused mechanistic relevance score 7-8; agents with indirect relevance score 4-6.
Safety Profile (20%)
Decades of clinical use, well-characterized side effect profile, documented drug-drug interactions, and safety in diverse populations. Agents with WHO Essential status and extensive use across continents score 9-10. Agents with established use but narrower databases score 7-8. Novel agents or agents with narrow therapeutic windows score 5-7.
Generic Availability (20%)
Global manufacturing infrastructure for generic formulations, WHO Essential Medicines List status, price point sustainability, and supply chain reliability. Sub-$0.20/month agents widely manufactured score 9-10 (Hernandez 2018, PMID:29710129). Agents under $1/month with established generics score 7-9 (PMID:29710129). Agents requiring specialized manufacturing or pricing >$2/month score 4-6 (PMID:29710129).
Evidence Strength (20%)
Published evidence specifically demonstrating diabetes-relevant effects in humans or translational models. Evidence from randomized controlled trials scores 9-10. Phase 2 efficacy data or multiple observational studies score 7-8. Mechanistic preclinical evidence or limited observational data score 4-6. Theoretical relevance only scores 1-3.
Equity Score (15%)
Suitability for deployment in low-resource settings including prevalence in LMICs, cold-chain independence, dosing simplicity, formulation flexibility, and integration with existing health systems. Agents available through WHO Essential Medicines procurement, requiring no cold-chain, and with 1-2x daily dosing in LMIC settings score 9-10. Agents requiring specialized monitoring or available primarily in high-income countries score 2-4.

Limitations

This analysis represents a computational screen and does not constitute clinical evidence for therapeutic use of agents outside their approved indications. Individual patient factors, drug interactions, contraindications, and regulatory approval status must be evaluated before any clinical application. The scoring framework reflects current understanding of diabetes pathophysiology and may be refined as new mechanistic evidence emerges.

Research Gaps Addressed

This screen directly addresses the following research priorities:

  • Affordable therapeutics in low-income countries: Identifying sub-dollar agents with diabetes-relevant mechanisms
  • Inflammation as a therapeutic target: Prioritizing NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitors and TNF-alpha antagonists
  • Beta cell preservation: Mapping agents with demonstrated C-peptide preservation or anti-apoptotic mechanisms
  • Immunomodulation in autoimmune diabetes: Identifying LADA and T1D-relevant agents for potential disease modification
  • Islet transplant tolerance: Prioritizing agents with mechanisms supporting graft survival and immune tolerance development
  • Combination therapy potential: Identifying complementary agents hitting different pathways for synergistic benefit
