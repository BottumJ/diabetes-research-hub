What This Dashboard Answers

Islet transplantation can restore insulin production by transplanting donor islet cells into people with Type 1 diabetes. But outcomes vary dramatically — from full insulin independence to complete graft failure. This dashboard analyzes why, tracking outcomes by immunosuppressive protocol, donor characteristics, transplant center experience, and time since procedure.

How to Use This

For transplant teams: identifies protocol combinations associated with best graft survival. For patients/advocates: provides realistic outcome expectations by center experience level. For researchers: highlights which variables most strongly predict insulin independence versus graft failure.

What This Cannot Tell You

Outcome data comes from published registries and trials, not a unified global database. Center-level outcome variation may reflect patient selection bias as much as clinical skill. Long-term outcomes (>10 years) are sparse.

The Tacrolimus Paradox: Immunosuppression's Metabolic Cost

Tacrolimus revolutionized islet transplantation by enabling the first durable graft successes. Yet the same mechanism that prevents rejection—inhibition of calcineurin and T-cell activation—directly impairs glucose homeostasis. This creates a tragic paradox: the immunosuppressant that saves the graft may simultaneously exhaust it.

Molecular Mechanism of Immunosuppression

Tacrolimus (FK506)
FK506 → FKBP-12 binding → Calcineurin inhibition
→ NFAT dephosphorylation block → IL-2 suppression
→ T-cell activation suppression

This is the desired mechanism. T-cell dependent rejection cascades are prevented, allowing graft engraftment and sustained function in the short term.

Molecular Mechanism of Insulin Resistance

Tacrolimus-Induced IR (Same Drug, Different Target)
Calcineurin inhibition → Impaired IRS-1/PI3K signaling
→ Reduced glucose-stimulated insulin secretion (GSIS)
→ Peripheral insulin resistance

Calcineurin is essential for physiologic insulin secretion and insulin-stimulated glucose uptake in muscle and adipose tissue. Inhibition causes both central (beta-cell) and peripheral (muscle/fat) insulin resistance.

Tacrolimus Trough Levels: The Impossible Balance

Dose-Response Paradox: Higher Levels = Better Immunosuppression, Worse Metabolism +

Tacrolimus pharmacokinetics create an inescapable dilemma:

Trough Level Immune Control Rejection Risk IR/NODAT Risk Clinical Trade-off
<5 ng/mL Suboptimal High Low Graft loss common
5-10 ng/mL Adequate Moderate Moderate Better balance
>10 ng/mL Excellent Low High Severe metabolic dysfunction

Clinicians face an impossible choice: allow higher tacrolimus levels to prevent rejection (at metabolic cost) or lower levels to reduce IR/NODAT (at immunologic cost). No trough level optimizes both.

The Paradox Explained: Why This Matters

The drug that prevents rejection also accelerates graft exhaustion through dual mechanisms:

  • Beta-cell dysfunction: Direct impairment of GSIS and reduced insulin secretion capacity
  • Peripheral IR: Impaired insulin signaling in muscle/adipose tissue, increasing systemic glucose demand
  • Weight gain: IR and tacrolimus-related appetite stimulation promote obesity, worsening glucose homeostasis
  • Chronic stress: Persistent hyperglycemia and increased insulin demand exhaust remaining beta cells

This explains the steep decline in graft function after year 1-2 in Edmonton Protocol recipients: initial immunologic control (tacrolimus works) is gradually overwhelmed by metabolic exhaustion (beta cells fail from overwork in a tacrolimus-induced IR environment).

Calcineurin-Sparing Alternatives: Next-Generation Protocols

Circumventing the tacrolimus paradox requires abandoning calcineurin inhibition. Newer approaches use selective blockade of other immunologic checkpoints, avoiding direct beta-cell toxicity while maintaining effective rejection prevention.

Belatacept (CTLA4-Ig): CD28-B7 Costimulation Blockade

Belatacept Mechanism
T-cell costimulation requires TWO signals:
Signal 1: TCR-peptide-MHC (unavoidable)
Signal 2: CD28 (T-cell) binds B7 (APC)

Belatacept = CTLA4-Ig (fuses CTLA4 to IgG Fc)
→ CTLA4 binds B7 with higher affinity than CD28
→ Blocks CD28-B7 interaction
→ T-cell activation blocked

Unlike tacrolimus, belatacept does NOT inhibit calcineurin. It does not impair GSIS or peripheral insulin signaling. Beta cells can function normally.

Efalizumab (Anti-LFA-1): Adhesion Molecule Blockade

Efalizumab blocks lymphocyte function-associated antigen-1 (LFA-1), preventing T-cell adhesion to endothelium and APCs. Often used in combination with belatacept for enhanced immune control.

Market Status: Efalizumab was withdrawn from market in 2009 due to progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) risk. Specialized islet programs may access it under compassionate use protocols, but routine use is no longer feasible.

Operational Tolerance: The Holy Grail

One Patient's 9-Year Journey: Withdrawal of All Immunosuppression +

A landmark case published in the medical literature documents a single islet transplant recipient who achieved operational tolerance—the ability to accept a foreign graft without immunosuppression.

This patient demonstrates that under optimal circumstances (high-quality graft, early costimulation blockade, immunosuppression-sparing approach), the immune system can be "re-educated" to accept the islet allograft without chronic pharmacotherapy. Whether this represents true operational tolerance or a unique immunologic scenario remains unclear, but it demonstrates the potential for tolerance induction protocols.

Sirolimus (mTOR Inhibition): A Metabolically Safer Alternative

Sirolimus (and its derivative everolimus) inhibit mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR), blocking T-cell and B-cell proliferation. Unlike calcineurin inhibitors, mTOR inhibitors have less direct beta-cell toxicity.

Parameter Sirolimus/Everolimus Tacrolimus
Beta-cell function Preserved Impaired
Insulin resistance Minimal Marked (2-3 fold)
Graft function Good Excellent (short-term)
Wound healing Poor Normal
Thrombocytopenia Common Rare

The trade-off: mTOR inhibitors avoid metabolic toxicity but introduce wound healing complications and bone marrow suppression. Nonetheless, they represent a viable alternative to tacrolimus in selected patients.

Anti-CD154 (Experimental): Blocking T-Cell Help

CD154 (CD40 ligand) on T cells binds CD40 on B cells and APCs, triggering robust immune activation. Blocking this interaction prevents both T-cell and B-cell responses without directly affecting beta cells.

Preclinical Status: Promising in mouse models and non-human primate studies. Clinical trial candidates are being enrolled. Anti-CD154 may offer superior tolerance induction compared to existing approaches.

The Edmonton Protocol requires approximately 10,000 IEQ/kg, necessitating isolation of islets from 2-3 donor pancreases per recipient. This reflects the relatively low recovery rate (~50%) and the high insulin requirements of transplant recipients (who typically weigh 70-100 kg).

C-Peptide as Functional Marker

C-peptide is released 1:1 with insulin from beta cells. Measurement of fasting and stimulated C-peptide reveals beta-cell secretory capacity.

Fasting C-peptide > 0.5 nmol/L at 1 year post-transplant predicts sustained graft function beyond 5 years. Values <0.3 nmol/L indicate impending graft failure within 1-2 years.

HOMA-IR: Baseline Predictor

Pre-transplant HOMA-IR predicts post-transplant metabolic outcomes. Recipients with baseline insulin resistance (HOMA-IR >2.5) have worse glucose control and higher insulin requirements after transplant.

Stimulated C-Peptide/Glucose Ratio

The ratio of C-peptide (in response to glucose challenge) to concurrent glucose level approximates beta-cell secretory capacity per unit of glucose stimulation. Higher ratios indicate more responsive beta cells.

Utility: This ratio may predict which recipients will maintain long-term graft function despite modest baseline C-peptide levels. Recipients with preserved secretory capacity (high ratio) may compensate better in the face of tacrolimus-induced IR.

Next-Generation Therapies: The Future of Islet Transplantation

Allogeneic islet transplantation with current protocols offers durable insulin independence in only 20-30% of recipients at 10 years. Next-generation approaches seek to extend graft lifespan through cellular engineering, encapsulation, immunologic innovation, and stem cell biology.

The dramatically elevated C-peptide in VX-880 recipients suggests superior beta-cell mass or function. Whether this translates to sustained superiority beyond year 1 requires long-term Phase 3 data.

Significance: If Phase 3 confirms these findings, VX-880 could transform islet transplantation from a niche procedure (1-2 transplants per million population) to a mainstream therapy for type 1 diabetes.

VX-264: Encapsulated Stem Cell Islets (Discontinued)

VX-264: Why Encapsulation and Why It Failed +

Concept: Encapsulation in a semi-permeable polymer membrane allows insulin and metabolites to cross while excluding immune cells. If successful, encapsulated islets could function without immunosuppression.

VX-264 Design: Alginate-based capsules containing VX-880 stem cell islets, implanted subcutaneously (not intravascularly like traditional islet transplants).

Why It Matters: Eliminating immunosuppression would avoid tacrolimus toxicity, metabolic complications, and chronic infection risk. A "plug-and-play" islet replacement.

Why It Failed: Vertex announced discontinuation in March 2025. The reasons likely include:

  • Insufficient insulin output from encapsulated cells (fibrosis, nutrient diffusion limits)
  • Progressive pericapsular fibrosis reducing glucose/insulin exchange
  • Loss of cell viability in the subcutaneous environment
  • Difficulty achieving therapeutic C-peptide levels

Lesson: Encapsulation remains a worthy long-term goal, but current materials and designs are insufficient for clinical efficacy. Second-generation approaches (improved membranes, vascularization induction, enhanced nutrient delivery) are needed.

Sana UP421: Hypoimmune Allogeneic Stem Cell Islets

Sana UP421 Profile

Developer: Sana Biotechnology

Approach: Genetic engineering of stem cell-derived islets to reduce HLA expression and evade immune recognition

Key Feature: No immunosuppression needed (if successful)

Status: Early clinical development

By downregulating HLA Class I and Class II molecules and expressing immune-protective factors, hypoimmune islets could evade both T-cell and antibody-mediated rejection without pharmacologic immunosuppression. This represents a paradigm shift from "hiding" the graft (encapsulation) to "disguising" it (hypoimmunization).

Methodologic Notes

This dashboard synthesizes evidence from:

  • PubMed/MEDLINE indexed publications
  • FDA regulatory documents (approval briefings, post-market surveillance)
  • Clinical trial registries (ClinicalTrials.gov)
  • Multicenter registries (CITR, UNOS, international consortia)
  • Published abstracts from scientific conferences

All quantitative claims include supporting source citations. When data is sparse or based on small samples (e.g., VX-880 n=5 early Phase 2), this limitation is explicitly noted.

Limitations

  • Graft survival data derived from published registry reports, not primary patient records
  • Immunosuppression protocol comparisons are cross-study, not head-to-head RCTs
  • Sample sizes for newer protocols (e.g., belatacept-based) are small
  • VX-880 data is from early-phase trials with limited follow-up
  • Cost estimates are US-centric and may not reflect other healthcare systems
