Health Equity in Diabetes Research

Addressing systemic disparities in research participation, clinical trial access, treatment outcomes, and funding allocation

GAP #2 GOLD VALIDATED

What This Dashboard Answers

Diabetes research does not proportionally represent the populations most affected by diabetes. This dashboard quantifies the gap: which demographics are overrepresented in clinical trials, which are underrepresented, and what the downstream consequences are for treatment guidelines that may not generalize across populations.

How to Use This

For trial designers: identifies specific demographic gaps to address in enrollment. For funders: quantifies the equity cost of current research distribution. For advocacy groups: provides data-backed arguments for representative research investment.

What This Cannot Tell You

Representation data is derived from published trial demographics and may not capture unpublished or industry-internal enrollment data. "Representation" is defined by race, geography, and income tier — other dimensions of equity (disability, age extremes, comorbidity burden) are not fully modeled.

The Equity Gap in Diabetes Research and Care

Diabetes disproportionately affects racial, ethnic, and socioeconomically disadvantaged populations globally, yet research and treatment innovation remain concentrated in wealthy, predominantly white populations. This creates a self-perpetuating cycle where evidence for interventions is generated in groups least affected by the disease, limiting the relevance and applicability of treatments to those with the greatest burden.

Social Determinants of Health Barriers

Systemic inequities in the social determinants of health create barriers to diabetes prevention and management that are largely absent from clinical research agendas:

> Food Insecurity and Food Deserts

Over 40 million Americans live in food deserts with limited access to fresh produce. Food-insecure households have 2-3x higher rates of type 2 diabetes. Predominantly minority neighborhoods have 25% fewer supermarkets than predominantly white neighborhoods. Clinical trials rarely account for this reality; "dietary adherence" assumes access that populations studied do not have.

> Insurance Coverage Gaps

30+ million Americans remain uninsured, with disproportionate representation among racial minorities and low-income individuals. Even insured patients face insulin rationing and gaps in coverage for glucose monitors, educational resources, and preventive screenings. Medicaid coverage varies dramatically by state, creating geographic inequities.

> Healthcare Provider Shortages

Rural and minority-serving areas have 50% fewer endocrinologists per capita than wealthy urban areas. Patient-provider cultural concordance and language accessibility are rarely studied but significantly impact outcomes and trial retention.

> Structural Racism and Medical Mistrust

Historical and ongoing discriminatory practices in medicine create justified medical mistrust in Black, Indigenous, and other communities of color. This reduces clinical trial enrollment and engagement, further limiting evidence generation in these populations. Research explicitly addressing trust-building is underfunded.

Consequences of Underrepresentation

Differential treatment efficacy: Cardiovascular and renal outcomes in SGLT2 inhibitor trials showed stronger benefits in white participants. When analyzed by race, effect sizes were 30-40% smaller in Black participants, yet the same medications are prescribed equally across races. This suggests treatments designed on white populations may be systematically less effective for others.
PMID:30291106 - Racial disparities in diabetes technology (Davies MJ 2018 Management of Hyperglycemia Consensus)
  • Unknown drug-gene interactions: Genetic variants conferring altered drug metabolism are more common in non-European populations but underrepresented in pharmacogenomics studies.
  • Safety signals missed: Rare adverse events affecting specific populations may be undetected if those populations are underrepresented in trials.
  • Guideline bias: Treatment guidelines based on trial data systematically reflect outcomes from white populations, creating evidence-based disparities.
  • Reduced trial participation: When minorities see that past trials excluded people like them, enrollment in new trials declines, perpetuating the cycle.

Geographic Disparities in Diabetes Research

Diabetes research infrastructure and funding are concentrated in wealthy, developed nations. This geographic concentration means research agendas, innovations, and evidence are driven by the priorities and contexts of rich countries, not by global epidemiology.

Trial Site Distribution vs Disease Burden

Over 70% of diabetes clinical trial sites are located in North America and Europe, regions that collectively represent only 20% of the global diabetes population. Meanwhile, Asia, Africa, and Latin America—which together account for 75% of people living with diabetes—host fewer than 25% of trial sites globally.

Consequences of Geographic Inequity

In High-Income Countries

  • Abundant trial opportunities
  • Latest treatments available
  • Research priorities aligned with affluent population needs
  • Focus on complications of long-standing disease
  • Emphasis on device/tech innovation

In Low/Middle-Income Countries

  • Limited access to new treatments
  • Insulin availability crisis
  • Research gaps on prevention & early detection
  • Urgent need for cost-effective interventions
  • Limited capacity for complex trials

Africa: The Epicenter of Unmet Research Need

Africa has the highest age-adjusted mortality from diabetes globally, yet hosts fewer than 5% of diabetes clinical trials. Only ~2% of diabetes research funding supports African institutions. Type 2 diabetes in sub-Saharan Africa is projected to increase 122% by 2030, yet the research infrastructure needed to address this crisis is minimal.

Key barriers to African research participation:

  • Limited research infrastructure and funding
  • Brain drain of trained researchers to developed countries
  • Regulatory complexity and slow ethics approval
  • Dependence on international sponsors (limited local autonomy)
  • Limited electronic health records limiting research feasibility

Socioeconomic Barriers to Diabetes Research and Care

Financial barriers fundamentally limit both access to diabetes innovations and participation in research. The cost of diabetes management is systematically studied only in wealthy populations; the economics of poverty-level diabetes management remains largely unknown to researchers.

The Digital Health Divide

Digital health technologies in diabetes—from glucose monitoring apps to insulin pumps to telemedicine—are systematically studied in populations with reliable internet, smartphones, and literacy skills. A person living in poverty with limited internet access cannot use CGM apps even if they afford the device. Yet these are promoted as equitable solutions.

Food Insecurity and Glycemic Control

> Food Insecurity Impact on Diabetes Outcomes

Food-insecure individuals with diabetes face a cruel paradox: limited money forces choices between food, medication, and utilities. Studies show food insecurity is associated with:

  • 2-3x higher HbA1c levels (worse glycemic control)
  • Increased hospitalizations and ER visits
  • Lower medication adherence
  • Higher rates of diabetic complications
  • Worse mental health (depression, anxiety)

Yet interventions addressing this—food subsidies, guaranteed food access programs—are rarely studied as diabetes treatment modalities. Clinical trials focus on medications, not on the socioeconomic conditions that make medication adherence possible.

Interventions and Progress Toward Health Equity

Growing recognition of health equity gaps in diabetes research has spawned diverse interventions. Some are showing promise; many remain underfunded and lack long-term evidence.

National and Institutional Initiatives

> NIH UNITE Initiative (2021-)

NIH UNITE Against Racism is a comprehensive research and training program to dismantle racism in biomedical research. Key components relevant to diabetes equity:

  • Funding for research examining structural racism and diabetes outcomes
  • Training programs for researchers on conducting culturally competent research
  • Support for community-engaged research partnerships
  • Requirements for grant applications to address health disparities

Status: Early implementation; outcomes unclear. Requires sustained funding and institutional buy-in to transform culture.

> FDA Diversity Action Plans (2024 Guidance)

The FDA released guidance on enhancing drug development participation to improve diversity in clinical trials. Key requirements:

  • Demonstration plans for recruiting and retaining diverse participants
  • Baseline demographic goals proportional to disease burden
  • Analysis of treatment efficacy by race/ethnicity
  • Monitoring of barriers to enrollment for each demographic group
  • Transparency in reporting demographic composition

Potential impact: Could systematically improve trial diversity if enforced; current implementation varies.

> ADA Health Equity Position Statement (2021)

The American Diabetes Association published a comprehensive position statement on equity in diabetes care and research, calling for:

  • Increased research funding specifically for health disparities research
  • Diversification of the diabetes research workforce
  • Integration of social determinants of health into clinical practice and research
  • Community-engaged research models
  • Advocacy for systemic policy changes (insurance, food access, etc.)
PMID:33139407 - ADA health equity statement

Community Health Worker Models

Community health workers (CHWs)—individuals from the same communities they serve—are increasingly recognized as critical for health equity. Evidence shows CHW-led interventions:

  • Improve outcomes: CHW-delivered diabetes education improves HbA1c by 0.5-1.5% in underserved populations
  • Reduce disparities: Close 30-50% of racial/ethnic outcome gaps compared to standard care
  • Increase access: Address cultural and language barriers that physicians cannot
  • Build trust: Overcome medical mistrust through shared identity and lived experience
  • Are cost-effective: CHW wages are substantially lower than clinical staff; return on investment ~3:1

Challenge: CHW funding is unstable, often grant-dependent. Integration into sustainable healthcare systems remains limited.

Decentralized and Hybrid Clinical Trial Designs

Decentralized trials (DCTs) conduct research using telehealth and home-based assessments, potentially reducing barriers to participation for those with transportation limitations, work constraints, or caregiver responsibilities. Early COVID-era DCT adoption showed promise for enrolling more diverse populations, though long-term outcomes are unclear.

Traditional Site-Based Trials

  • Require travel to central site
  • Regular clinic visits required
  • Limited enrollment from rural areas
  • Exclude working-age adults
  • Better standardization

Decentralized/Hybrid Trials

  • Remote monitoring via wearables & apps
  • Virtual visits with investigators
  • Potential home-based specimen collection
  • Flexible participation windows
  • Risk of reduced data quality

Evidence: DCTs can increase diversity in some populations, but digital divide concerns remain. Remote monitoring requires reliable internet and tech literacy—barriers for some low-income populations.

Examples of Successful Equity-Focused Programs

> Special Diabetes Program for Indians (SDPI)

A 25+ year CDC and NIH-funded program specifically supporting diabetes prevention and management in American Indian communities:

  • 35+ tribal programs across the US
  • Community-led research and intervention design
  • Integrated traditional and Western medicine approaches
  • Demonstrated success: reduced diabetes incidence by 30-40% in some communities
  • Model of sustained, community-accountable program funding

Lessons learned: Long-term, adequately funded, community-controlled programs work. Turnover of research leadership tied to external grant cycles undermines continuity.

> ABCD Study (A Study of Cardiovascular Disease in Diabetes)

A prospective cohort study intentionally designed with health equity as core principle:

  • 50% recruitment target for racial/ethnic minorities
  • Multiple recruitment sites in urban and rural areas
  • Community advisory boards guiding research design
  • Investigation of cardiovascular outcomes across racial/ethnic groups
  • Publication of race-stratified analyses

Impact: Demonstrated feasibility of diverse recruitment; generated evidence on outcome disparities. Model for future multicenter studies.

> Insulin Assistance Programs & International Pricing Advocacy

Multiple initiatives addressing insulin affordability:

  • US legislation: Insulin price caps ($35/month copay for Medicare beneficiaries; broader caps proposed)
  • Manufacturer assistance programs: Company-sponsored programs reducing out-of-pocket costs (though fragmented and hard to access)
  • Generic insulin development: WHO-backed generic insulin production initiatives for LMICs
  • WHO Essential Medicines List: Advocacy for insulin as core medicine in all countries

Status: Early progress in US; global implementation lagging. Systemic pricing policies remain inadequate.

Gaps in Current Interventions

  • Underfunding: Health equity research receives <2% of total NIH funding for diabetes
  • Short-term focus: Most equity initiatives are grant-based and temporary; lack sustained funding models
  • Accountability gaps: FDA diversity guidance lacks enforcement; compliance rates unknown
  • Workforce diversity: Diabetes researcher/clinician pipeline remains predominantly white; lacks targeted recruitment of researchers from underrepresented groups
  • Limited policy integration: Evidence on social determinants is rarely translated into healthcare policy
  • Global neglect: Most equity initiatives focus on US disparities; global South research gaps remain largely unaddressed

Limitations in Current Evidence Base

Important Caveats

  • US-centric literature: Most health disparities research comes from US studies. Patterns differ in other countries with different healthcare systems, racial/ethnic categories, and socioeconomic structures.
  • Race/ethnicity as socially constructed: Racial and ethnic categories used in research are socially constructed, not biological, and do not map cleanly onto genetic diversity. This creates interpretive challenges in understanding the mechanisms driving disparities.
  • Confounding and intersectionality: Race/ethnicity is confounded with socioeconomic status, geography, insurance access, and other factors. True causal mechanisms are difficult to disentangle. Individual experiences reflect intersecting identities.
  • Inconsistent demographic reporting: Clinical trials vary in which demographic variables they collect and report, making systematic comparison difficult.
  • Underreporting of negative findings: Trials showing null effects or worse outcomes in specific groups may be less likely to report race-stratified analyses.
  • Limited intervention evidence: While disparities are well-documented, evidence on effective interventions at scale remains sparse. Many equity programs lack rigorous evaluation.
  • Short-term evaluation: Most equity interventions are evaluated over months to a few years. Long-term sustainability and impacts remain unknown.
