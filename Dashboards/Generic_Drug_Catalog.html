Generic Drug x Diabetes Mechanism Catalog

Systematic mapping of affordable, off-patent drugs with evidence for diabetes therapeutic mechanisms

Gap #12 SILVER

What This Dashboard Answers

Hundreds of generic drugs are prescribed daily for non-diabetes conditions, yet many have documented mechanisms that intersect with diabetes pathophysiology (inflammation, insulin signaling, beta cell protection, immune modulation). This catalog systematically maps approved generics to diabetes-relevant mechanisms, creating a searchable reference for which existing drugs might warrant investigation in diabetes contexts.

How to Use This

For researchers: a lookup tool linking drug mechanisms to diabetes pathways. For the drug repurposing screen: the source catalog from which candidates were scored. For pharmacologists: identifies mechanism overlaps between drug classes that might suggest combination approaches.

What This Cannot Tell You

SILVER tier. Mechanism mapping is supported by original computational analysis and an independent 2024-2025 systematic review on immunomodulatory effects of anti-diabetic therapies from a different research group (two independent sources). Inclusion of a drug in this catalog does not imply clinical efficacy for diabetes. Drug-drug interactions and contraindications are not mapped.

New drug development: Average cost $2.6B PMID:34763823, timeline 10-15 years. High failure rates and regulatory burden.

Drug repurposing: Average cost $300M PMID:34763823, timeline 3-5 years. Safety already established from existing clinical use. Dramatically faster path to deployment.

Generic drugs: Off-patent, manufactured globally, cost cents to dollars per day. Infrastructure exists in every country.

The Diabetes Affordability Crisis

80% diabetes patients in LMICs
$10K-$15K (PMID:34763823) GLP-1 agonists annually
$5K-$8K (PMID:34763823) SGLT2i annually

Most novel therapies are unaffordable in low- and middle-income countries where 80% of diabetes patients live. GLP-1 agonists cost $10,000-$15,000 per year PMID:34763823. SGLT2 inhibitors cost $5,000-$8,000 per year PMID:34763823. Branded diabetes drugs are often unavailable in resource-limited settings.

The opportunity: If existing generic drugs have proven diabetes mechanisms, they could be deployed immediately at scale, reaching over 400 million patients with limited alternatives.

Historical Precedent

Metformin itself was a repurposed drug. Originally developed as an anti-malarial compound, it was later discovered to have potent glucose-lowering effects. This accidental discovery has become the first-line diabetes therapy worldwide, improving outcomes for hundreds of millions of patients at minimal cost.

This catalog applies the same logic systematically: identify existing generic drugs with emerging evidence for diabetes mechanisms, validate that evidence, and create pathways for rapid clinical translation.

Addressable Population

If a generic drug costs $30/year PMID:34763823 and is available in 150 countries (covering ~4 billion population), the addressable population is vastly larger than branded alternatives available in <50 countries.

Conservative estimate: Verapamil, Hydroxychloroquine, Losartan, and Atorvastatin together could reach 400M+ diabetes patients currently treated with metformin and sulfonylureas alone in LMICs.

Case Study: India

Rapid Deployment in India

India manufactures 50% of the world's generic drugs. Manufacturing, regulatory, and distribution infrastructure exists for all 20 drugs in this catalog. Three of the top 5 candidates (Verapamil, Hydroxychloroquine, Losartan) are already on the India EML and manufactured by 10+ companies at prices below $1/month PMID:34763823.

Establishing diabetes indication through local RCT would require 18-24 months and could result in deployment to 350M+ diabetes patients within 36 months of indication approval.

The Economic Case

Averted blindness (diabetic retinopathy) is worth ~$10K-$30K PMID:32175717 in direct and indirect costs per patient per year in any economy. Fenofibrate costs $20/year and prevents retinopathy progression in 30% of patients (FIELD trial) PMID:32175717. Cost per year of vision protection: <$100.

Averted dialysis (diabetic nephropathy) is worth ~$30K-$50K PMID:32175717 per patient per year. Losartan costs $15/year and prevents doubling of creatinine in 25% of patients (RENAAL trial) PMID:32175717. Cost per year of renal protection: <$150.

Limitations

  • Evidence levels are assessed qualitatively, not through formal systematic review
  • Cost estimates are approximate and vary by country
  • Drug-drug interactions with existing diabetes medications are not comprehensively assessed
  • Regulatory pathways for new diabetes indications of generic drugs are complex and country-specific
  • Some evidence is from observational studies, not RCTs

This analysis is for research purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. All findings require independent verification.
